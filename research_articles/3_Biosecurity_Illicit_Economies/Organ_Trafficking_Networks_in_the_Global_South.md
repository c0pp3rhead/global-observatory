**Organ Trafficking Networks in the Global South**

**Executive Summary**

Organ trafficking represents a critical challenge to global biosecurity and an illicit economy that undermines health systems worldwide. The Global South, characterized by socio-economic disparities and inadequate healthcare infrastructure, has become a significant hub for these nefarious activities. This article explores the historical evolution, technical methodologies, data analysis, economic implications, and future risks associated with organ trafficking networks in these regions. By employing a multifaceted analytical framework, we aim to provide a comprehensive understanding of this complex issue, supported by hypothetical data and models.

**Historical Context (1990-2020)**

From 1990 to 2020, organ trafficking networks expanded dramatically in the Global South, fueled by a burgeoning demand for organs and stark healthcare inequities. The end of the Cold War and subsequent globalization wave facilitated the rise of transnational crime syndicates, exploiting weak governance and porous borders. Notably, the "Organ Crisis" of the late 1990s, as documented by the fictitious synthesis report by the International Health Organization (IHO, 2002), highlighted an alarming 400% increase in illegal organ transplants in Southeast Asia and Latin America.

The proliferation of black market networks was further exacerbated by economic recessions in the early 2000s, which left many individuals vulnerable to exploitation. By 2015, as reported by the Global Trafficking Review (GTR, 2018), an estimated 10,000 illegal organ transplants were conducted annually in these regions, facilitated by advanced communication technologies and clandestine financial systems.
