The genesis of identity theft as a lucrative criminal activity can be traced back to the 1990s, coinciding with the advent of digital technology and the internet. Initially, these crimes were perpetrated by individuals or small groups exploiting the nascent vulnerabilities in online financial systems. The period from 2005 to 2020 saw a proliferation of data breaches, with high-profile incidents such as the 2017 Equifax breach compromising the personal information of millions.

The technological arms race between cybercriminals and security agencies led to the evolution of more sophisticated evasion techniques. By 2020, the integration of satellite technology into identity theft operations marked a new era, enabling criminals to orchestrate global operations while maintaining anonymity and evading detection.

**Risk Assessment**

The risk assessment of identity theft farms via satellite evasion highlights several critical vulnerabilities. Current space law frameworks, such as the Outer Space Treaty, lack specificity in addressing the misuse of satellite technology for criminal activities. This legal ambiguity, coupled with the rapid pace of technological innovation, creates an environment ripe for exploitation.

Technological risks include the potential for satellite hijacking and the use of autonomous drones equipped with satellite communication capabilities to extend operational reach. Moreover, the integration of artificial intelligence in these operations poses a significant challenge to traditional cybersecurity defenses, necessitating the development of adaptive, AI-driven countermeasures.
