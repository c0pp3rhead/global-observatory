**Thermodynamic Efficiency of Mycelium Composites during Solar Particle Events**

**Engineering Abstract**

In the pursuit of sustainable materials for space habitats, mycelium composites have emerged as a promising candidate due to their lightweight, biodegradable, and self-healing properties. However, the exposure of these materials to intense radiation events, such as Solar Particle Events (SPEs), challenges their structural integrity and thermodynamic efficiency. This research note investigates the thermodynamic performance of mycelium composites under SPE conditions, focusing on their ability to maintain structural and thermal stability. The study aims to quantify the potential of mycelium as a viable construction material in extraterrestrial environments, particularly during unpredictable solar activities.

**System Architecture**

The system under consideration consists of a mycelium composite panel integrated into a hypothetical lunar habitat module. The primary components include the mycelium matrix, reinforcing agents (e.g., cellulose fibers), and a protective outer coating designed to mitigate radiation exposure. Inputs to the system encompass solar radiation flux, temperature gradients, and mechanical stressors, while outputs measure structural integrity, thermal conductivity, and degradation rates.

The mycelium composite's architecture (dimensions: 1.5 m x 1.5 m x 0.05 m) is designed to optimize thermal insulation while maintaining a minimal mass profile (approximately 20 kg/m²). The panel's thermodynamic efficiency is evaluated under simulated SPE conditions, characterized by proton flux densities reaching up to 1000 MeV and temperature variations from -150°C to 120°C.

**Mathematical Framework**

The thermodynamic performance of the mycelium composites is assessed using a combination of heat transfer equations and radiation shielding models. The heat equation, a form of the Navier-Stokes equations adapted for thermal analysis, is employed to model the temperature distribution across the composite:

\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha \nabla^2 T + \frac{Q}{\rho c_p} \]

where \( T \) is the temperature (K), \( \alpha \) is the thermal diffusivity (m²/s), \( Q \) is the heat generation per unit volume (W/m³), \( \rho \) is the density (kg/m³), and \( c_p \) is the specific heat capacity (J/kg·K).

Radiation shielding effectiveness is quantified using the Beer-Lambert Law, considering the attenuation coefficient (\( \mu \)) of the mycelium composite:

\[ I = I_0 e^{-\mu x} \]

where \( I \) is the radiation intensity (W/m²) after passing through the material, \( I_0 \) is the initial intensity (W/m²), and \( x \) is the thickness of the material (m).

To account for mechanical stress under radiation pressure, the stress-strain relationship follows Hooke's Law, adapted for anisotropic materials:

\[ \sigma = E \epsilon \]

where \( \sigma \) is the stress (MPa), \( E \) is the modulus of elasticity (MPa), and \( \epsilon \) is the strain.

The self-healing properties of mycelium, activated by thermal cycling, contribute to a 20% recovery in mechanical strength post-radiation exposure.

**Failure Modes & Risk Analysis**

Despite promising results, several potential failure modes warrant consideration. The primary risk involves the degradation of the mycelium matrix due to prolonged exposure to high-energy protons, leading to a loss in structural integrity. Risk mitigation strategies include the incorporation of additional cellulose fibers and the application of advanced polymer-based coatings to enhance radiation resistance.

Thermal expansion under rapid temperature changes poses another risk, potentially causing delamination within the composite layers. This issue can be addressed through the development of hybrid composites with tailored thermal expansion coefficients to match lunar environmental conditions.

Lastly, the biological nature of mycelium introduces variability in material properties, necessitating stringent quality control measures as outlined in IEEE Standard 1900.60 for sustainable biomaterials.

**Conclusion**

Mycelium composites present a viable material for space habitats, offering resilience against SPE-induced thermal and radiation stressors. The integration of engineered additives and protective coatings enhances their performance, positioning them as a sustainable alternative for extraterrestrial construction. Further research should focus on long-term material behavior and the development of real-time monitoring systems to ensure structural integrity in dynamic space environments.
