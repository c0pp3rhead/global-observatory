**Redox Potential Stabilization of Electrodialysis Reversal Systems under Artificial Gravity**

**Engineering Abstract**

The advent of long-term space habitation necessitates the development of reliable and efficient life-support systems. One critical challenge is managing waste and recycling water in microgravity environments. Electrodialysis reversal (EDR) systems have emerged as a promising technology for desalination and ion separation in space. However, the application of EDR under artificial gravity conditions, such as those found in rotating space habitats, presents unique challenges, notably the stabilization of redox potential. This study examines the stabilization of redox potential in EDR systems under artificial gravity, ensuring optimal performance and longevity of the system. By investigating the influences of centrifugal forces on ion transport and redox reactions, we aim to provide a comprehensive understanding and practical solutions to enhance system reliability.

**System Architecture**

The EDR system designed for artificial gravity environments consists of several key components: an ion exchange membrane stack, electrodes, a power supply, and control systems. The ion exchange membranes, composed of cation and anion exchange polymers, are arranged in alternating layers to form cell pairs. Each cell pair is separated by spacers that facilitate fluid flow. The electrodes, typically made from titanium coated with ruthenium oxide, are positioned at the ends of the membrane stack.

Inputs to the system include a saline feed solution and a direct current (DC) power supply, which typically operates between 1 to 3 kW, depending on the system size. The outputs include desalinated water and a concentrated brine solution. The system operates under an artificial gravity environment, generated by rotating the habitat at a rate of 1 to 2 rpm, creating a centrifugal force equivalent to 0.3 to 1 g.

**Mathematical Framework**

The mathematical framework for this study builds on the Nernst-Planck equation, which models ion transport through membranes. The primary equation governing ion flux, \(J_i\), is given by:

\[ J_i = -D_i \frac{dC_i}{dx} + \frac{z_i F}{RT} C_i \frac{d\phi}{dx} - u C_i \]

where \(D_i\) is the diffusion coefficient, \(C_i\) is the ion concentration, \(x\) is the spatial coordinate, \(z_i\) is the ion valence, \(F\) is Faraday's constant (96485 C/mol), \(R\) is the universal gas constant (8.314 J/(mol·K)), \(T\) is the temperature in Kelvin, \(\phi\) is the electric potential, and \(u\) is the fluid velocity induced by artificial gravity.

Redox potential stabilization is analyzed using the Butler-Volmer equation to describe the electrode kinetics:

\[ j = j_0 \left( \exp\left(\frac{\alpha_a n F \eta}{RT}\right) - \exp\left(-\frac{\alpha_c n F \eta}{RT}\right) \right) \]

where \(j\) is the current density, \(j_0\) is the exchange current density, \(\alpha_a\) and \(\alpha_c\) are the anodic and cathodic transfer coefficients, \(n\) is the number of electrons involved in the reaction, and \(\eta\) is the overpotential.

**Failure Modes & Risk Analysis**

Failure modes in EDR systems under artificial gravity include membrane fouling, electrode degradation, and system leaks. Membrane fouling, primarily due to scalant deposition, is mitigated by periodic polarity reversal and chemical cleaning using dilute HCl (0.1 M). Electrode degradation, particularly the loss of catalytic activity, is addressed through the use of robust coatings like iridium oxide, with a predicted lifespan exceeding five years under continuous operation.

System leaks, exacerbated by the centrifugal forces, are minimized by employing ISO 9001 certified seals and pressure vessels rated up to 0.5 MPa. The risk analysis, conducted in accordance with IEEE 1609 standards, highlights the need for redundant systems and real-time monitoring to detect and mitigate failures promptly.

In conclusion, the stabilization of redox potential in EDR systems under artificial gravity is feasible with appropriate design considerations and control strategies. This study provides critical insights and practical solutions for implementing EDR technology in space habitats, contributing to the sustainability of long-term space missions.
