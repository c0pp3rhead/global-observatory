### Gas-Liquid Interfacial Area of Anaerobic Digesters during Solar Particle Events

#### Engineering Abstract

The utilization of anaerobic digesters in extraterrestrial environments, such as on spacecraft or lunar bases, presents unique challenges due to the influence of cosmic phenomena. This research note investigates the impacts of Solar Particle Events (SPEs) on the gas-liquid interfacial area within anaerobic digesters, a key parameter affecting the efficiency of biogas production. The study delves into the alteration of interfacial dynamics induced by SPEs, which can potentially lead to suboptimal methane yields. By applying advanced fluid dynamics modeling and simulations under varying SPE intensities, this work aims to provide a rigorous understanding of the operational limitations and adaptations required for biosystems engineering in space.

#### System Architecture

The anaerobic digester system under consideration is designed for space applications, capable of processing organic waste generated by a crew of six astronauts (approximately 2 kg/person/day). The system consists of a cylindrical digester with a volume of 1 m³, maintained at a temperature of 35°C to optimize mesophilic microbial activity. The architecture integrates sensors for real-time monitoring of pressure (up to 0.5 MPa), temperature, and gas composition (CH₄, CO₂, H₂S).

Inputs to the system include organic waste and inoculum, and outputs consist of biogas and digestate. The digester is equipped with a gas-liquid interface monitoring system employing laser-induced fluorescence (LIF) to assess interfacial area changes. The system's design adheres to ISO 22000 standards for food safety and IEEE 1233 for system engineering processes.

#### Mathematical Framework

The mathematical analysis focuses on the Navier-Stokes equations to model fluid dynamics within the digester under the influence of SPEs. The equations are modified to incorporate a source term representing the energy input from SPEs, modeled as a Gaussian distribution with a peak intensity of 10^-3 kW/m². The change in interfacial area is linked to the energy input through the capillary number (Ca), defined as:

\[ Ca = \frac{\mu U}{\sigma} \]

where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity (0.001 Pa·s), \( U \) is the characteristic velocity induced by SPEs, and \( \sigma \) is the surface tension (0.072 N/m). The interfacial area \( A \) is given by:

\[ A = A_0 \left(1 + \alpha \cdot Ca\right) \]

where \( A_0 \) is the initial interfacial area, and \( \alpha \) is a proportionality constant derived empirically.

#### Failure Modes & Risk Analysis

Potential failure modes include structural integrity loss of the digester due to pressure fluctuations and degradation of sensor accuracy from radiation exposure. Risk analysis, conducted using Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA), identifies the highest risks as follows:

1. **Pressure Fluctuations**: Excessive gas production may lead to pressure spikes. Mitigation includes installing pressure relief valves and employing adaptive control algorithms (IEEE 1451) for real-time adjustments.

2. **Radiation Damage**: SPE-induced radiation can degrade sensor performance. Implementing radiation-hardened electronics and redundant sensor arrays can reduce this risk.

3. **Microbial Inhibition**: SPEs may inhibit microbial activity. Developing radiation-resistant microbial strains through genetic modification presents a long-term solution.

In conclusion, understanding the interplay between SPEs and anaerobic digester dynamics is crucial for optimizing biogas production in space. Future work will focus on experimental validation of the simulation results and exploration of advanced materials for shielding and enhancing system resilience. This research provides a foundation for the development of robust biosystems engineering strategies for future space missions.
