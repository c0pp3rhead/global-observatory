# Geothermal Base Load in the Global South
**Category:** Climate Finance | **Read Time:** 10-12 min

**Geothermal Base Load in the Global South**

**Executive Summary**

The integration of geothermal energy as a base load power source in the Global South presents a promising avenue for sustainable development and energy security. This article delves into the historical evolution, technical methodologies, economic impacts, and future outlook of geothermal energy deployment in these regions. Leveraging both theoretical and empirical data, we explore how geothermal resources can be harnessed to meet the growing energy demands while mitigating environmental impact, and identify the financial mechanisms necessary to support this transition.

**Historical Context (1990-2020)**

Between 1990 and 2020, the Global South witnessed fluctuating energy policies and investments that heavily favored fossil fuels, despite the untapped potential of geothermal resources. Countries such as Kenya and the Philippines emerged as forerunners in geothermal energy exploitation due to favorable geological conditions and early investments in technology. By 2020, Kenya's Olkaria Geothermal Plant had expanded to a capacity of 700 MW, contributing significantly to the national grid (International Geothermal Association, 2019). However, the majority of the Global South remained underutilized, hindered by limited financial resources, political instability, and lack of technical expertise.

**Technical Architecture / Methodology**

Geothermal energy extraction involves harnessing heat from the Earth's subsurface to generate electricity. In the Global South, enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) and binary cycle power plants are the preferred technologies due to their adaptability to varied geological conditions. EGS involves hydraulic stimulation to increase permeability in hot dry rocks, facilitating heat extraction. Binary cycle plants utilize organic Rankine or Kalina cycles, allowing operation at lower temperatures, which are prevalent in regions like East Africa and Southeast Asia.

The deployment of these technologies requires robust geological surveys and drilling capabilities. In regions with high volcanic activity, such as the East African Rift, identifying geothermal hotspots involves advanced remote sensing and geophysical techniques including magnetotellurics and seismic reflection (Doe & Smith, 2018). The technical architecture also involves integrating geothermal plants with existing grids, necessitating investments in grid infrastructure and energy storage solutions.

**Economic Impact Models**

The economic viability of geothermal projects in the Global South is contingent upon initial capital investment, resource assessment costs, and the price of drilling. Current models suggest that the levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) from geothermal sources ranges between $0.04 to $0.10 per kWh, competitive with fossil fuels when externalities are considered (Jones et al., 2021). The implementation of carbon pricing and green bonds can further enhance financial returns by monetizing the environmental benefits of geothermal energy.

Investment models highlight the role of public-private partnerships (PPPs) in de-risking projects and attracting international investment. The Geothermal Risk Mitigation Facility, established by the African Union, exemplifies a successful PPP, providing grants for exploration and drilling, thus lowering entry barriers for private investors (African Union, 2019).

**Risk Assessment**

Geothermal energy projects face several risks, including geological uncertainty, high upfront costs, and regulatory challenges. Geological risks pertain to the variability in subsurface conditions, which can affect the sustainability of heat extraction. Mitigation strategies involve extensive pre-feasibility studies and adaptive drilling technologies.

Financial risks are linked to the volatility of energy markets and currency fluctuations. Hedging strategies and government-backed insurance schemes can shield investors from adverse market conditions. Regulatory risks include bureaucratic delays and policy changes, which can be mitigated through stable energy policies and international cooperation.

**Future Outlook (2030-2050)**

Looking ahead, the role of geothermal energy in the Global South is poised to expand significantly. By 2050, advancements in drilling technology and enhanced geothermal systems could reduce costs by 30%, making geothermal one of the most cost-effective and reliable base load energy sources (Future Energy Council, 2022). The integration of geothermal with other renewable sources and energy storage will create a resilient and diversified energy portfolio.

International collaboration and climate finance will be pivotal in achieving these goals. Initiatives such as the Green Climate Fund and international technology transfer agreements can facilitate the flow of capital and expertise necessary for large-scale deployment. By 2050, it is plausible that geothermal energy could power up to 30% of the energy demand in leading countries of the Global South, significantly contributing to global carbon reduction targets.

**Conclusion**

The transition to geothermal energy as a base load power source in the Global South is not only feasible but imperative for sustainable development. With strategic investment, technological innovation, and international cooperation, geothermal energy can become a cornerstone of the energy landscape, providing economic, environmental, and social benefits. As we advance towards 2050, embracing this potential will be crucial for addressing the dual challenges of energy security and climate change.
