# Techno-Economic Analysis (TEA) of Synthetic Fuel Refineries for Stranded Asset Valuation

## Engineering Abstract

The advent of synthetic fuels as a sustainable alternative to conventional fossil fuels has prompted significant interest in utilizing stranded assets—such as abandoned oil fields and underutilized industrial sites—for synthetic fuel production. This research note presents a techno-economic analysis (TEA) of synthetic fuel refineries, assessing their viability and the potential economic value of these stranded assets. The analysis encompasses both the engineering design and financial valuation, providing a comprehensive framework for stakeholders. The study explores the integration of cutting-edge technologies in biosystems engineering, focusing on energy efficiency, production capacity, and economic return on investment. This note aims to provide a quantitative baseline for future development in this emerging sector.

## System Architecture

The synthetic fuel refinery system is designed to convert biomass and carbon dioxide into liquid hydrocarbons through a series of chemical and biochemical processes. The primary components include:

1. **Feedstock Processing Unit**: Converts raw biomass (e.g., lignocellulosic material) into fermentable sugars. The unit operates at a throughput of 500 kg/day, using enzymatic hydrolysis facilitated by cellulase enzymes.

2. **Gasification Unit**: Biomass is thermochemically converted into syngas (CO + H2) in a high-pressure (3 MPa) and high-temperature (850°C) environment.

3. **Fischer-Tropsch Synthesis Reactor**: Converts syngas into liquid hydrocarbons using a cobalt-based catalyst, operating at a pressure of 2 MPa and 220°C. The reactor's efficiency is optimized to a conversion rate of 85%.

4. **Product Upgrading Unit**: Refines the raw hydrocarbons into synthetic fuels, achieving a final output of 1000 liters/day of diesel-equivalent fuel.

5. **Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) System**: Captures CO2 emissions from the process, reducing the net carbon footprint. The system captures 90% of the CO2 produced, equivalent to 200 kg/day.

Energy inputs are primarily derived from renewable sources, ensuring a minimal carbon footprint. Overall energy consumption is estimated at 1 MW, with a net production efficiency of 40%.

## Mathematical Framework

The economic viability of the synthetic fuel refinery is assessed using a combination of process engineering models and financial valuation techniques. The key equations and logic include:

- **Mass and Energy Balances**: Governing the conversion processes in the gasification and synthesis units. For instance, the energy balance equation is given by:
  \[
  \Delta H_{\text{reaction}} = \sum n_i H_i^{\circ} - \sum n_j H_j^{\circ}
  \]
  where \( \Delta H_{\text{reaction}} \) is the change in enthalpy, and \( H_i^{\circ} \) and \( H_j^{\circ} \) are the standard enthalpies of reactants and products, respectively.

- **Economic Analysis**: Utilizes the Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of Return (IRR) to evaluate financial performance. The Black-Scholes model for option pricing is adapted to assess the value of potential future revenue streams from stranded assets.

- **Risk Assessment**: Incorporates Monte Carlo simulations to evaluate uncertainties in feedstock costs, product prices, and technological advancements.

## Simulation Results

Simulation results indicate that the proposed refinery design can produce synthetic fuel at a competitive cost of $1.2 per liter, assuming a biomass feedstock price of $50/ton. The NPV of the project, over a 20-year lifespan, is estimated at $15 million, with an IRR of 12%. These figures suggest a promising return on investment, particularly in scenarios with rising fossil fuel prices.

## Failure Modes & Risk Analysis

Potential failure modes for the synthetic fuel refinery include:

- **Feedstock Supply Variability**: Fluctuations in biomass availability can impact production schedules. Strategies such as diversifying feedstock sources and implementing advanced inventory management systems are recommended.

- **Technological Breakdown**: Critical equipment failures, particularly in the gasification and synthesis units, can disrupt operations. Regular maintenance and the adoption of ISO 9001 standards for quality management are essential to mitigate this risk.

- **Market Volatility**: Changes in the market price of synthetic fuels and carbon credits can affect economic viability. Hedging strategies and flexible pricing models are advised to manage financial exposure.

- **Regulatory Changes**: Evolving environmental regulations may necessitate additional investments in emissions control technologies. Continuous monitoring of regulatory trends and proactive compliance measures are essential.

This research note underscores the potential for synthetic fuel refineries to transform stranded assets into economically viable energy solutions. By employing a rigorous techno-economic framework, stakeholders can make informed decisions that align with both engineering feasibility and financial sustainability. Future work will focus on optimizing the integration of renewable energy sources and further refining the economic models to enhance predictive accuracy.
