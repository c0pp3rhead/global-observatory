# Techno-Economic Analysis (TEA) of Molten Salt Storage for Carbon Offset Verification

## Engineering Abstract

The increasing urgency to address climate change necessitates innovative solutions for carbon offset verification. Molten salt storage (MSS) emerges as a promising technology in the context of renewable energy systems, providing a stable medium for thermal energy storage, which is crucial for balancing supply and demand. This research note delves into the techno-economic analysis of MSS systems, assessing their viability for carbon offset verification. We explore the integration of MSS with biosystems engineering processes, evaluating its potential in reducing carbon footprints while ensuring economic feasibility. Through rigorous simulation and analysis, we aim to provide a comprehensive understanding of the MSS system's operational dynamics and financial implications.

## System Architecture

The MSS system architecture comprises several critical components: a solar field, a heat exchanger, a molten salt storage tank, and a power generation unit. The system utilizes a binary nitrate molten salt mixture (60% NaNO₃ and 40% KNO₃) as the thermal storage medium. The solar field, equipped with parabolic trough collectors, captures solar energy, converting it into thermal energy at a rate of 500 kW. This thermal energy is then transferred to the molten salt via a heat exchanger, operating at an efficiency of 85%.

The molten salt storage tank is designed to operate under a pressure of 0.3 MPa, maintaining the salt at a temperature range of 290°C to 565°C. This temperature range ensures a high energy density, with a storage capacity of up to 5,000 kWh per cycle. The power generation unit, operating on a Rankine cycle, converts thermal energy back into electrical energy with a thermal efficiency of 40%. The system's design allows for continuous operation, with an uptime of 95%, ensuring reliable energy supply and carbon offset verification.

## Mathematical Framework

The techno-economic analysis of the MSS system involves several mathematical models and equations. The energy balance equation for the system is given by:

\[ Q_{\text{in}} = Q_{\text{stored}} + Q_{\text{loss}} + Q_{\text{out}} \]

Where \( Q_{\text{in}} \) is the thermal energy input from the solar field, \( Q_{\text{stored}} \) is the energy stored in the molten salt, \( Q_{\text{loss}} \) accounts for thermal losses, and \( Q_{\text{out}} \) is the energy output from the power generation unit.

The thermal efficiency (\( \eta_{\text{thermal}} \)) of the system is calculated using:

\[ \eta_{\text{thermal}} = \frac{Q_{\text{out}}}{Q_{\text{in}}} \]

For economic analysis, the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) is employed, defined by:

\[ \text{LCOE} = \frac{C_{\text{total}}}{E_{\text{total}}} \]

Where \( C_{\text{total}} \) is the total life-cycle cost of the system, encompassing capital, operational, and maintenance costs, and \( E_{\text{total}} \) is the total energy output over the system’s lifetime.

Carbon offset is calculated using the formula:

\[ \text{CO}_2\text{ offset} = \left( \frac{E_{\text{total}}}{\text{kWh}} \right) \times \text{emission factor (kg CO}_2/\text{kWh)} \]

## Failure Modes & Risk Analysis

Despite the promising potential of MSS systems, several failure modes warrant consideration. Thermal degradation of molten salt, particularly at elevated temperatures beyond 600°C, poses a risk to system integrity. The risk of salt freezing, especially in cold climates, necessitates robust insulation and heating mechanisms to maintain operational temperatures.

Corrosion of system components, especially at high temperatures, is another critical concern. The use of corrosion-resistant materials and coatings, in compliance with ISO 9223 standards, is imperative to mitigate this risk. Additionally, system scalability and integration with existing energy infrastructures require careful planning to avoid bottlenecks and inefficiencies.

Risk analysis, conducted using the Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) methodology, identifies potential failure points and quantifies their impact on system performance. The analysis highlights the importance of proactive maintenance and real-time monitoring to ensure system reliability and longevity.

In conclusion, the MSS system presents a viable solution for carbon offset verification within the biosystems engineering framework. Its techno-economic advantages, coupled with the capacity for significant carbon reduction, position it as a pivotal technology in the transition to a sustainable energy future. Further research and development, particularly in materials science and thermal management, will enhance system performance and broaden its applicability across diverse environmental conditions.
