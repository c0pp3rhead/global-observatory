# Geothermal Base Load under Net-Zero Targets

## Executive Summary

The transition to net-zero carbon emissions is a critical global priority that requires innovative solutions to meet the growing energy demands sustainably. Geothermal energy, as a reliable and sustainable power source, holds significant potential for contributing to base load power under net-zero targets. This research article explores the historical context, technical architecture, economic impacts, and future prospects of geothermal energy in the context of achieving net-zero emissions by 2050. Through a comprehensive analysis, we aim to provide insights into how geothermal energy can be leveraged to meet these ambitious climate goals.

## Historical Context (1990-2020)

The period from 1990 to 2020 saw significant advancements in renewable energy technologies, driven by increasing awareness of climate change and the need for sustainable energy sources. Geothermal energy, which harnesses the Earth's internal heat, has been utilized for electricity generation and direct heating applications. In the early 1990s, geothermal energy contributed approximately 0.1% to global electricity production, primarily in regions with abundant geothermal resources such as Iceland, New Zealand, and the western United States (International Energy Agency, 2021).

Throughout the 2000s, technological advancements in drilling and resource assessment led to increased geothermal capacity, with countries like Kenya and the Philippines emerging as major contributors. By 2020, geothermal energy accounted for about 0.3% of global electricity generation, with an installed capacity of 15.4 GW (World Geothermal Congress, 2020). Despite its modest contribution, the stable and continuous nature of geothermal energy makes it an attractive candidate for base load power in a renewable-dominated grid.

## Technical Architecture / Methodology

The technical architecture of geothermal power plants typically involves the extraction of hot water or steam from geothermal reservoirs located several kilometers beneath the Earth's surface. This geothermally heated fluid is then used to drive turbines connected to electricity generators. There are three main types of geothermal power plants: dry steam, flash steam, and binary cycle.

1. **Dry Steam Plants**: These are the simplest and oldest types of geothermal power plants. They directly utilize steam from geothermal reservoirs to drive turbines. Typical examples include the Geysers in California.

2. **Flash Steam Plants**: These plants are more common and operate by depressurizing hot water from the reservoir, causing it to flash into steam. This steam is then used to power turbines.

3. **Binary Cycle Plants**: These plants use a secondary working fluid with a lower boiling point than water. The geothermal fluid heats this secondary fluid in a heat exchanger, and the vaporized secondary fluid drives the turbine. Binary cycle plants are more versatile and can utilize lower temperature resources.

Innovations in Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) have further expanded the potential of geothermal energy by allowing the development of geothermal resources in areas lacking natural permeability. EGS involves creating artificial reservoirs by injecting water into hot dry rocks, enhancing the feasibility of geothermal projects in diverse geological settings.

## Economic Impact Models

The economic viability of geothermal energy is influenced by several factors, including upfront capital costs, resource availability, and market conditions. Geothermal projects require significant initial investment for exploration, drilling, and plant construction, with typical costs ranging from $2,500 to $5,000 per kW installed capacity (Geothermal Energy Association, 2019). However, once operational, geothermal power plants offer low operating costs and stable electricity prices.

Economic impact models suggest that scaling up geothermal energy can contribute to job creation and economic development in regions with suitable resources. A study by the Renewable Energy Policy Network (2022) estimates that expanding geothermal capacity to meet 5% of global electricity demand by 2050 could create over 1 million direct and indirect jobs.

Furthermore, the integration of geothermal energy into the energy mix can enhance grid stability and reduce reliance on fossil fuels, contributing to energy security and resilience against volatile fuel prices.

## Risk Assessment

Despite its potential, geothermal energy development is not without risks. Exploration and drilling activities carry geological and financial uncertainties. Resource assessment errors can lead to suboptimal project outcomes, while seismic activity induced by drilling poses environmental and safety challenges.

Financial risks are also significant, with high capital costs potentially deterring investment in regions with limited access to financing. Policy and regulatory frameworks play a crucial role in mitigating these risks by providing incentives, de-risking mechanisms, and clear guidelines for project development.

Technological risks include the potential for scaling issues in Enhanced Geothermal Systems, where maintaining reservoir performance over time requires continuous monitoring and management.

## Future Outlook (2030-2050)

Looking ahead, the role of geothermal energy in achieving net-zero targets is poised to expand. By 2050, projections suggest that geothermal capacity could reach 70 GW, contributing up to 3% of global electricity generation (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2023). The development of geothermal resources in regions with untapped potential, such as parts of Africa, Latin America, and Asia, will be critical to this growth.

Advancements in drilling technologies, such as laser drilling and smart sensing, are expected to reduce costs and enhance resource identification. The integration of geothermal energy with other renewable sources, such as solar and wind, could further optimize energy systems and provide continuous power supply.

In conclusion, geothermal energy represents a vital component of the global energy transition. Its ability to provide reliable base load power, coupled with advancements in technology and supportive policy frameworks, positions it as a key player in the quest for net-zero emissions. As we move towards 2050, harnessing the Earth's internal heat offers a promising path to a sustainable energy future.
