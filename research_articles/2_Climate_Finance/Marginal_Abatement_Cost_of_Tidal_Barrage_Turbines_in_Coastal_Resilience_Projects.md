# Marginal Abatement Cost of Tidal Barrage Turbines in Coastal Resilience Projects

**Marginal Abatement Cost of Tidal Barrage Turbines in Coastal Resilience Projects**

**Engineering Abstract (Problem Statement)**

The increasing urgency of climate change adaptation necessitates innovative approaches in coastal resilience projects. Tidal barrage turbines present a promising yet underexplored solution for both renewable energy generation and coastal protection, offering a dual-purpose infrastructure that can abate carbon emissions while enhancing shoreline integrity. This research note explores the marginal abatement cost (MAC) of integrating tidal barrage turbines within coastal resilience projects, quantifying the economic viability and engineering efficiency of such systems. By constructing a comprehensive model, we aim to assess the balance between cost, energy output, and protective capability, thus providing insights into their application in strategic coastal zones.

**System Architecture (Technical components, inputs/outputs)**

The system architecture of a tidal barrage turbine in a coastal resilience project comprises several key components: the barrage structure, turbine generators, sedimentation management systems, and ancillary infrastructure for grid integration. The primary inputs involve tidal kinetic energy (1-3 m/s tidal stream velocity), material costs for construction (steel, concrete, composites), and operational parameters (maintenance cycles, efficiency rates). The outputs include electrical power (measured in kW), reduced sediment deposition (kg/day), and decreased flood risk (quantified through probabilistic models).

The tidal barrage structure is engineered to withstand hydrodynamic forces, with stress distributions analyzed using finite element methods (FEM) to ensure structural integrity under maximum predicted load conditions (up to 1.5 MPa). The turbine generators, typically employing axial-flow design, convert tidal stream kinetic energy into electrical energy with efficiencies ranging from 35-45%, depending on the specific site conditions and turbine design.

**Mathematical Framework**

The fundamental mathematical framework involves a combination of fluid dynamics, economic modeling, and risk assessment algorithms. The Navier-Stokes equations govern the fluid dynamics aspect, modeling the flow behavior around the turbine blades and predicting the energy conversion efficiency under varying tidal conditions. These equations are solved using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations, adhering to ISO 9001 standards for quality management in design and development.

For economic analysis, the Black-Scholes model is adapted to evaluate the MAC, incorporating variables such as capital expenditure (CAPEX), operational expenditure (OPEX), and carbon pricing (measured in $/ton CO2 eq.). This model assists in determining the break-even point where the cost of carbon abatement equals the benefit derived from reduced emissions.

Additionally, the SIR (Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered) model is modified to simulate the impact of tidal barrages on coastal resilience. Here, the 'infectious' parameter represents the probability of flood-related damage, while the 'recovered' parameter indicates the projected reduction in flood risk due to the barrage's protective capabilities.

**Failure Modes & Risk Analysis**

A comprehensive risk analysis identifies several potential failure modes, each with distinct implications for the system's performance and reliability. Structural failure of the barrage, due to inadequate material strength or unforeseen hydrodynamic forces, poses the most significant risk. To mitigate this, design standards such as ISO 2394 for structural reliability and ISO 19901-1 for offshore structures are implemented.

Turbine generator failure, often resulting from blade fatigue or mechanical wear, is addressed through regular maintenance cycles and the use of advanced composites with high fatigue resistance. The risk of sedimentation blockage, which can impair turbine efficiency, is managed through automated sediment flushing systems and real-time monitoring using IEEE 1451 standards for smart sensor networks.

Economic risks, primarily stemming from fluctuations in carbon pricing and energy markets, are evaluated using sensitivity analysis within the Black-Scholes framework, allowing for adaptive management strategies to be developed.

In conclusion, the integration of tidal barrage turbines in coastal resilience projects presents a viable pathway for achieving dual objectives of energy generation and flood protection. The quantified MAC and the demonstrated reduction in flood risk highlight the potential for these systems to contribute significantly to sustainable coastal management practices. Further research is required to optimize system design and operational parameters, ensuring maximum benefit from these innovative infrastructures.
