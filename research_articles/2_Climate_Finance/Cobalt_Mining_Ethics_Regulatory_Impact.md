This article delves into the history, methodologies, and future outlook of cobalt mining with an emphasis on ethical considerations and regulatory impacts. **Historical Context (1990-2020)**

The turn of the 21st century marked a significant surge in demand for cobalt, driven by the proliferation of electronic devices and electric vehicles (EVs). Between 1990 and 2020, global cobalt production increased from approximately 22,000 metric tonnes to nearly 140,000 metric tonnes annually. This period was characterized by a concentration of mining activities in the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), which accounted for over 60% of global supply by 2020. The lack of stringent regulatory frameworks during this era led to widespread unethical practices, including child labor and unsafe working conditions, raising significant humanitarian and environmental concerns.

The primary risks associated with cobalt mining include geopolitical instability, market volatility, and environmental degradation. Geopolitical risk is particularly pronounced in the DRC, where political unrest can disrupt supply chains. Market volatility is driven by fluctuations in demand from the EV sector, while environmental risks are linked to the potential for soil and water contamination from mining activities. **Future Outlook (2030-2050)**

Looking towards the mid-21st century, the demand for cobalt is expected to continue its upward trajectory, fueled by advancements in battery technology and renewable energy storage solutions. However, the landscape of cobalt mining is likely to undergo significant transformation. 

By 2030, we envisage the widespread adoption of cobalt-free battery technologies, reducing dependency on this critical mineral. Concurrently, advancements in deep-sea mining and asteroid mining could provide alternative sources of cobalt, albeit with their own ethical and environmental challenges.

From a regulatory perspective, we anticipate the establishment of an international cobalt certification system akin to the Kimberley Process for diamonds, which would standardize ethical sourcing practices globally. Furthermore, increased investment in recycling technologies could lead to a circular economy for cobalt by 2050, minimizing environmental impact and ensuring a sustainable supply chain.
